A Quantum Computing primer

2016-06-06 | Modified: 2020-04-28

Abstract

In this paper, an overview of quantum computers will be addressed; fundamental quantum mechanical properties, recent advancements and potential uses for the upcoming technology. I have focused on one recent development of the qubit from a team of researchers at the University of New South Wales (UNSW) who are using a phosphorus atom imbedded in a chip of silicon-28 as the qubit. (Borghino, 2015)

Introduction

The digital age of computing has started to diverge. Traditional computers are becoming much smaller; scientists are developing molecular computers which are only 100s of nanometres wide. In addition to the shrinking of technology, scientists are looking towards quantum mechanics and its strange properties to create a new way of processing information.

Traditionally classical computers store information in two states, 0 or 1. However, quantum computers store information as 0, 1 or as a state of both 0 and 1. (Altepeter, 2010) This allows the potential for more powerful computers with same amount of 'classical bits'.

Recent advances in creating a qubit

Qubits can be a multitude of things from the polarization of a photon, the energy levels of an ion or the nuclear spin of an atom. (unknown, n.d.) The most promising candidate has been utilised by a team of researchers at the University of New South Wales (UNSW). They are harnessing the spin of an electron in a phosphorus atom. (Borghino, 2015)

Electron spin

The direction of an electrons spin state is caused by the interaction of the electrons magnetic field and the external magnetic field applied to it. When the electrons magnetic field aligns with the external magnetic field the lowest energy state is achieved (spin down). To get it into spin up, a pulse of microwaves are fired at the electron at a specific frequency. In addition, if you stop the pulse at a specific time, the electron will be in a superposed state of both up and down.

However, the property of the electron spin means that only discrete values of an electron spin exists- namely spin up and spin down. This means when the direction is measured, it will be in a classical state of up or down. The choice using electron spin over other means is highlighted below by the researchers at the UNSW.

"Silicon electron 'spins' also have very long 'coherence times': this means data encoded on the spin can remain there for longer periods than it would in most materials, before it is scrambled and lost. This is important for performing successful calculations." (University of New South Wales, 2015)

Superposition

The state of both 0 and 1 is a consequence of superposition. Sub-atomic particles such as electrons and protons can exist in a superposition of these two states (or wave of probability). (Mastin, 2009) It is helpful to use mathematical notation shown to describe the phenomenon.

$$\left. \ \mathbf{|\psi} \right\rangle\mathbf{= a}\left. \ \mathbf{|0} \right\rangle\mathbf{+}\left. \ \mathbf{b|1} \right\rangle$$

Where a and b are coefficients of finding the electron in either \(\left. \ \mathbf{|0} \right\rangle\) or \(\left. \ \mathbf{|1} \right\rangle\) and \(\mathbf{\ }\left. \ \mathbf{|\psi} \right\rangle\) is the qubit (wave function).

As previously mentioned, the wave function exists as the sum of possible probabilities before being measured. As soon as the qubit is measured the wave function collapses to one of the two states. (unknown, n.d.)

Entanglement

Two qubits have 4 quantum states, \(\mathbf{|0\rangle|0\rangle}\), \(\left| \mathbf{0} \right\rangle\left| \mathbf{1} \right\rangle\mathbf{+ \ |1\rangle|0\rangle}\), \(\left| \mathbf{0} \right\rangle\left| \mathbf{1} \right\rangle\mathbf{- |1\rangle|0\rangle}\) and \(\mathbf{|1\rangle|1\rangle}\). The second state listed known as the singlet state has very peculiar properties; any measurement of one half of the singlet state will give a completely random result. On the other hand, when both qubits are measured, they result in opposite spin direction- it seems as if the electrons seem to know about the others state. (Mastin, 2009) They become entangled and described as one system rather than two separate bodies.

This important property of the quantum world is known as entanglement. 2 entangled qubits provide 4 pieces of information- the probability of finding the qubit in each of the 4 states. In comparison 2 classical bits gives us 2 pieces of information- the state of each bit. Consequently, the amount of information provided through qubits is \(\mathbf{2}^{n}\) classical bits. With only 300 qubits, all in an entangled state of superposition, the number of 'classical' information exceeds the amount of particles in the universe.

Applications

Since quantum computers exploit the parallelisms of quantum phenomena, one major use of the technology would be to crack the RSA encryption. Factorising large numbers that would take classical computers billions of years could potentially be done in a few minutes. Furthermore, our understanding of diseases would rapidly increase due to quantum computers. (Freiberger, 2015)

Overcoming problems

Unfortunately, getting a fully functioning quantum computer has many problems. A large amount of them stem from the qubit. The qubit needs to be in an environment free of external factors which will cause decoherence. Quantum computers are cooled to just above 0Â°K as thermal energy at room temperature renders measuring electron spin completely. Decoherence also occurs if the material surrounding the qubit has a magnetic nucleus (Tyryshkin, 2011). Fortunately, the team at the university of new south wales are using silicon-28 as it doesn't cause decoherence. (Avaliani, 2002)

Conclusion

Scientists hope to improve qubit coherence time and create stronger interactions with its environment. In 10 years' time, the team at UNSW hope to create a quantum computer with 10+ qubits which is stable enough to work at room temperature. However, we are still at the beginning of a new approach to computing and data management. The future of quantum computing is still in its infancy; the potential to harness quantum phenomenon has yet to be fully understood.
